• Summary
  This manuscript asks an important question and uses an ambitious stylized ABM to connect self-determination theory, social contagion, and post-labor policy. The topic is relevant for JASSS. In its current form,
  however, the paper is not reliable enough for publication because several headline claims are inconsistent with the supplied implementation and, in multiple places, inconsistent with one another.

  Recommendation
  Reject in current form; reconsider only after a full model audit and complete rerun of the experiments. The problems are not cosmetic. They affect the core process being modeled, the interpretation of the speed
  experiments, and the credibility of the reported quantitative results.

  Major Issues

  1. Displacement is not persistent in the implementation. Each step the model draws a fresh random set of displaced agents, rather than keeping already displaced agents displaced: model.py:259, model.py:262,
     model.py:267. That means “automation” is repeated reshuffling of who lacks a role, not cumulative labor displacement. This directly undermines the interpretation of transition dynamics, chronic meaning loss, and
     intervention timing in paper_draft.md:174, paper_draft.md:206, and paper_draft.md:283.
  2. The speed experiments are internally impossible as documented. The manuscript says runs last 80 timesteps: paper_draft.md:74. It then defines gradual automation as 0.01/step, reaching 80% in 80 steps:
     paper_draft.md:211. But it also reports gradual results at 95% post-labor: paper_draft.md:216. In the code, post-labor rises only by automation_speed per step: model.py:253, model.py:255. A speed of 0.01 cannot
     reach 0.95 in 80 steps.
  3. The manuscript contains unresolved numerical contradictions. UBI-only sink is 0.515 in the abstract: paper_draft.md:15, but 0.639 in Results: paper_draft.md:253. Roles-only sink is 0.458 in the abstract:
     paper_draft.md:17, but 0.388 in Results: paper_draft.md:249. Baseline collapse at 80% is 2%: paper_draft.md:174, methods_appendix.md:124, yet zero virtual quality at 80% is 100%: paper_draft.md:189, maximum
     collectivism at 80% is still 95%: paper_draft.md:225, and the Nauru-like 80% case is 100%: paper_draft.md:287. These read as version mixing, not interpretable scenario differences.
  4. Intervention effects are not identified cleanly. The manuscript presents UBI, roles, and fairness as distinct interventions: paper_draft.md:86, paper_draft.md:240, and later calls the structure additive:
     paper_draft.md:321. But in code, fairness is partly a direct function of UBI: model.py:211, model.py:213. “UBI only” is therefore not UBI only. Likewise “roles” alters both economic_role and competence directly:
     model.py:115, model.py:270. If the scenarios are binary-coded, UBI also sets economic_role = 0.30, while virtual-role uptake requires economic_role < 0.3: model.py:152, model.py:270. That makes the very strong
     UBI+virtual results hard to interpret: paper_draft.md:196, paper_draft.md:246.
  5. The model description does not fully match the code. The main text defines meaning as a weighted sum: paper_draft.md:104, paper_draft.md:106. But the implementation also subtracts contagion and adds resilience:
     methods_appendix.md:44, model.py:159, model.py:165, model.py:166. The Results section also invokes a contribution_to_nonplayers term: paper_draft.md:200, but no such term exists in the supplied model; the code uses
     only economic_role * 0.8 + virtual_role * 0.1: model.py:158.
  6. Reporting and validation are below the standard needed for a JASSS article. The sweep table gives counts of runs but not the exact level vectors or scenario matrix needed to reproduce the 17,050-run design:
     paper_draft.md:139, paper_draft.md:143, paper_draft.md:148. The Nauru/Gulf comparison is presented as “historical validation” in Results: paper_draft.md:285, paper_draft.md:291, but is acknowledged later to be post
     hoc and confounded: paper_draft.md:336. That is not adequate external validation.

  Model Validity
  Internal validity is weak because the implemented process does not match the narrative of cumulative job loss. Nonpersistent displacement: model.py:262. Memoryless archetypes: methods_appendix.md:92. Coupled
  interventions: model.py:213.

  Construct validity is also limited. Many key quantities are explicit researcher choices rather than empirically grounded parameters, including the status and contribution weights: methods_appendix.md:57,
  methods_appendix.md:58, the 8:1 economic-to-virtual ratio: methods_appendix.md:64, methods_appendix.md:65, and several convenience-calibrated agent/network settings: methods_appendix.md:7, methods_appendix.md:17. That
  is acceptable for a stylized ABM only if the paper stays qualitative; this manuscript instead makes sharp threshold and policy-ranking claims: paper_draft.md:13, paper_draft.md:240.

  Statistical validity is not yet persuasive. The paper emphasizes a critical region around 80-90% and even a “critical point ~88%”: paper_draft.md:13, but sensitivity analysis probes only 0.80 and 0.95:
  paper_draft.md:154, methods_appendix.md:118. Results are then reported as exact percentages and zeros without uncertainty intervals: paper_draft.md:180, paper_draft.md:231, paper_draft.md:243.

  Missing Literature
  The framing of work, identity, and well-being in paper_draft.md:31 and paper_draft.md:42 should engage the unemployment/latent-deprivation literature: Jahoda, Fryer, McKee-Ryan et al., and Paul & Moser. That literature
  is directly about the psychosocial functions of work and is more relevant here than SDT alone.

  The paper’s “meaning” claims in paper_draft.md:42 and paper_draft.md:299 need the meaningful-work literature, especially Rosso et al. and Steger/Allan. Otherwise “meaning infrastructure” reads as an intuitive label
  rather than a construct linked to established scholarship.

  The automation review at paper_draft.md:37 is too thin. It should include Acemoglu & Restrepo and Arntz, Gregory, and Zierahn, which give a more nuanced picture of task automation and displacement.

  The contagion framing at paper_draft.md:64 should move beyond Christakis and Fowler’s obesity paper and engage complex contagion and network-diffusion work such as Centola and Macy.

  The Nauru/Gulf comparison in paper_draft.md:285 and paper_draft.md:289 needs resource-curse and rentier-state scholarship, such as Ross and Beblawi/Luciani, to avoid reading as selective historical analogy.

  For JASSS specifically, the methods sections in paper_draft.md:72, paper_draft.md:139, and methods_appendix.md:163 should engage ABM protocol and validation literature: Grimm et al. on ODD/ODD+D, pattern-oriented
  modeling, and validation work by Windrum et al. or Edmonds & Moss.

  This report is based on source inspection. I did not reproduce runs locally because the current environment is missing numpy/Mesa dependencies.
